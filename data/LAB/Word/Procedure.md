## Experiment 1: Linear Measurement

### Overview

Three precision measuring instruments are used to measure linear dimensions: Vernier Calliper, Digital Vernier Calliper, and Micrometer.

### Objectives

* Study and understand operation of precision measuring instruments
* Measure dimensions using three different instruments
* Compare accuracy levels of different instruments
* Calculate average and error analysis

### Apparatus

| Instrument               | Specification         | Least Count |
| ------------------------ | --------------------- | ----------- |
| Vernier Calliper         | L-shaped frame        | 0.02 mm     |
| Digital Vernier Calliper | Electronic display    | 0.01 mm     |
| Micrometer               | Screw & nut mechanism | 0.01 mm     |

### Theory

#### Vernier Calliper

* **Principle:** Two scales of slightly different sizes
* **LC Formula:** LC = Main Scale Division / Total Vernier Divisions = 1/50 = 0.02 mm
* **Reading Formula:** Total = MSR + (LC × Coinciding Vernier Division)

#### Micrometer

* **Principle:** Screw and nut mechanism
* **LC Formula:** LC = Pitch / Divisions on Thimble = 0.5 / 50 = 0.01 mm
* **Reading Formula:** Total = Barrel Reading + (LC × Thimble Division) - Zero Error

#### Precision Requirements

* **Precision Instruments:** Error ≤ 0.0025 mm
* **Non-Precision Instruments:** Error > 0.25 mm
* **5 Requirements:** Accuracy, durability, ease of use, readability, robustness

### Procedure

#### Vernier Calliper Measurement

1. Hold firmly with object between measuring tips
2. Ensure tips make full contact with object surfaces
3. Apply light pressure (do not use ratchet)
4. Record main scale reading (MSR)
5. Identify coinciding vernier division
6. Calculate: Total = MSR + (LC × Vernier Division)
7. Take three readings from different positions
8. Calculate average

#### Digital Vernier Calliper Measurement

1. Zero display by pressing zero/on button with jaws closed
2. Place object between measuring jaws
3. Slide jaws until gentle contact is achieved
4. Read measurement directly from display (LC: 0.01 mm)
5. Take three readings
6. Calculate average

#### Micrometer Measurement

1. Check for zero error with spindle closed
2. Place object between anvil and spindle
3. Rotate thimble until object is gently clamped (use ratchet)
4. Record main scale reading (MSR)
5. Identify thimble scale division at reference line
6. Calculate: Total = MSR + (LC × Thimble Division) - Zero Error
7. Take three readings
8. Calculate average

* Always apply consistent, light pressure

* Take multiple readings for accuracy

* Micrometer is most accurate of the three

## Experiment 2: Calibration of Dial Gauge

### Overview

Periodic calibration ensures dial gauges maintain accuracy and reliability in precision measurement.

### Objectives

* Understand calibration principles
* Perform complete dial gauge calibration
* Identify worn-out zones
* Determine discardation point

### Apparatus

| Item               | Specification                          |
| ------------------ | -------------------------------------- |
| Dial Gauge         | L.C. 0.01 mm                           |
| Calibration Tester | Slip gauge holders                     |
| Slip Gauges        | 0.5 to 10 mm range, 83 pieces, Grade 0 |

### Theory

#### Why Calibration is Required

* Stringent manufacturing tolerances demand verification
* Grade dial by accuracy
* Determine worn-out zones
* Inspect after repairs
* Ascertain discardation point

#### Calibration Principle

* Compare against standard slip gauges
* Identify systematic errors
* Adjust zero and scale corrections
* Verify repeatability and linearity

### Procedure

1. **Setup:** Keep dial gauge on calibration tester on holding stand
2. **Zero Setting:** Touch plunger to platform, set pointer to zero
3. **Small Displacement:** Use knob for small displacement, re-zero pointer
4. **0.5 mm Test:** Place 0.5 mm slip gauge, reading should be 0.5 mm
   * If not: adjust screw under dust cap
5. **1 mm Test:** Add 1 mm slip gauge, repeat verification
6. **2 mm Test:** Add 2 mm slip gauge, repeat verification
7. **Incremental Testing:** Repeat until 10 mm total
8. **Reverse Testing:** Remove one-by-one, repeat from 10 mm to 0 mm
9. **Deviation Analysis:** Check for inconsistencies
10. **Adjustment:** Tighten/loosen screw under protection cap for deviations

### Calibration Stages

* **Ascending:** 0.5 → 1.0 → 1.5 → ... → 10 mm
* **Descending:** 10 mm → ... → 1.0 → 0.5 mm
* **Repeatability Check:** Verify same readings on ascent and descent

### Key Points

* Must use Grade 0 slip gauges for accuracy
* Check for hysteresis (different readings ascending vs descending)
* Adjust under dust protection cap only
* Record deviations at each step
* Document discardation threshold

## Experiment 3: Floating Carriage Micrometer

### Overview

Measurement of screw thread parameters including major diameter and effective diameter using precision floating carriage micrometers.

### Objectives

1. Study floating carriage micrometer operation
2. Measure effective diameter of thread plug gauge
3. Measure major diameter of thread plug gauge
4. Study two-wire method for effective diameter
5. Study best wire size calculation

### Apparatus

| Instrument                   | Specification            |
| ---------------------------- | ------------------------ |
| Floating Carriage Micrometer | Standard specimen method |
| Tread Measuring Wire         | Diameter 0.895 mm        |
| Master Cylinder              | Diameter 10 mm           |
| Steel Balls                  | Rolled center type       |

### Theory: Screw Thread Terminology

#### Basic Terms

| Term                                | Definition                                                      |
| ----------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Screw Thread                        | Continuous helical groove on cylinder/cone                      |
| Crest                               | Prominent part joining thread sides                             |
| Root                                | Bottom of groove between adjacent threads                       |
| Flank                               | Straight surface between crest and root                         |
| Pitch                               | Axial distance between corresponding points on adjacent threads |
| Lead                                | Axial advance in one complete revolution                        |
| Depth of Thread                     | Distance between crest and root perpendicular to axis           |
| Thread Angle                        | Angle between flanks in axial plane                             |
| Major Diameter (D)                  | Diameter of cylinder touching thread crests                     |
| Minor Diameter                      | Diameter of cylinder touching thread roots                      |
| Effective Diameter (Pitch Diameter) | Cylinder where thread and space widths are equal                |

#### Advanced Terms

| Term                       | Definition                                            |
| -------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------- |
| Lead Angle                 | Angle of helix at pitch line with perpendicular plane |
| Helix Angle                | Angle of helix at pitch line with axis                |
| Virtual Effective Diameter | Pitch diameter of enveloping thread with full depth   |
| Multiple Start Thread      | Two or more helical grooves equally spaced            |

### Procedure: Major Diameter Measurement

**Using Ordinary Micrometer:**

1. Prepare setting gauge (master cylinder) ≈ same diameter as thread major diameter
2. Place setting gauge between micrometer anvils
3. Record reading R1 (over setting gauge)
4. Replace with screw thread
5. Record reading R2 (over thread)
6. Calculate: **Major Diameter = D + (R2 – R1)**
   * Where D = diameter of setting gauge
7. Measure at 2-3 positions for taper detection
8. Measure at 2-3 angular positions for ovality detection

**Using Bench Micrometer (For Greater Accuracy):**

1. Calibrated setting cylinder placed between anvils
2. Record R1 = micrometer reading over setting cylinder
3. Replace with threaded workpiece
4. Record R2 = micrometer reading over thread
5. Calculate: **Major Diameter = D + (R2 – R1)**
6. Multiple position and angular measurements required

### Procedure: Effective Diameter Measurement

#### Method 1: Thread Micrometer Method

* Simplest and rapid method
* Special contact points match thread form
* Male contact in anvil, female contact in spindle
* Direct reading of effective diameter
* Shows drunken thread variation

#### Method 2: One Wire Method

1. Place one wire between two threads at one side
2. Micrometer contacts crests on other side
3. Measure standard gauge: record d1
4. Replace standard with thread: record d2
5. Calculate: **Effective Diameter = D = (d1 – d2)**
   * Where D = size of setting gauge
   * If d2 > d1: use positive sign
   * If d2 < d1: use negative sign

#### Method 3: Two Wire Method

1. Select wires of suitable diameter
2. Place wires between standard and micrometer anvils
3. Record R1 = micrometer reading (over standard + wires)
4. Replace standard with screw thread
5. Record R2 = micrometer reading (over thread + wires)
6. Calculate: **T = S – (R1 – R2)**
   * Where S = diameter of standard
7. Calculate: **E = T + P**
   * Where E = effective diameter, P = constant for thread

**Constant Calculation (P):**

* Whitworth threads: **P = 0.9605p – 1.1657d**
* Metric threads: **P = 0.866p – d**
* Where p = pitch of thread, d = diameter of wire

#### Method 4: Three Wire Method (Most Accurate)

1. Select three wires of equal precise diameter
2. Place 2 wires on one side, 1 wire on other side in thread grooves
3. Measure distance M over outer surface of wires with micrometer
4. Calculate: **M = E + Q**
   * Where E = effective diameter, Q = constant
5. Calculate Q: **Q = W(1 + cosec θ) – (p/2) cot θ**
   * Where W = diameter of wire, θ = thread half angle, p = pitch
6. Calculate: **E = M – Q = M – [W(1 + cosec θ) – (p/2) cot θ]**

### Best Wire Size Calculation

**Principle:** Wire makes contact at pitch line (perfect effective diameter measurement)

**Derivation:**

* Line OP (wire center to contact points) ⊥ flank
* In triangle OAP: sin(90–x) = AP/OP
* Where x = half included angle of thread
* OP = AP/cos x = AP sec x
* AP = p/4 (on pitch line)
* **Best Wire Diameter = 2 × (p/4) × sec x = (p/2) sec x**

### Key Points

* Light pressure essential to avoid crest deformation

## Experiment 4: Profile Projector & Tool Maker's Microscope

### Overview

Two precision inspection instruments for magnified viewing and measurement of small components with high accuracy.

#### Profile Projector

**Principle:** Uses lenses and light beams to magnify profiles of small shapes

**Optical Arrangement:**

* Point A: Light source at focus of collimating lens B
* Lens B: Collimating lens sends parallel light
* Focus E: Focus by projection lens D

* Comparison with accurate drawings at magnification scale
* Numerical assessment of deviation
* Non-contact measurement
* Complete profile visibility

**Magnification Scales:**

* 10x: For larger components
* 20x: Standard magnification
* 50x: For very small features

#### Tool Maker's Microscope

**Construction:**

* Heavy hollow base A: Houses lighting unit
* Table B: Mounted on balls C for vertical axis movement
* Blade movement: Horizontal in perpendicular directions (e-f)
* Micrometer drums: Project from back for table movement
* Column b: Rotates about axis XX

* Guide ways j: Allow clamping with knob k
* Eyepiece unit l: Different magnifications available
* Lighting system: Via lenses and reflectors

**Workpiece Support:**

* Between centres for rotating components

### Procedure: Profile Projector

4. **Table Movement:** Use micrometer drums for horizontal movement (perpendicular directions)
5. **Magnification:** Select appropriate eyepiece magnification

## Experiment 5: Surface Roughness Measurement

### Overview

Two methods for analyzing and measuring surface texture: Optical flat interferometer and electronic stylus roughness tester.

### Objectives (Part A - Interferometer)

* Study principle of optical interference
* Understand optical flat construction
* Identify surface irregularities using fringe patterns
* Analyze surface flatness using interference bands

### Objectives (Part B - Roughness Tester)

* Measure surface roughness using stylus method
* Calculate CLA and RMS values
* Understand surface texture elements
* Determine manufacturing process adequacy

### Apparatus

| Part | Instrument       | Specification                   |
| ---- | ---------------- | ------------------------------- |
| A    | Optical Flat     | One side flat, Grade I or II    |
| A    | Light Source     | Sodium vapor (monochromatic)    |
| B    | Stylus Tester    | Diamond stylus, digital display |
| B    | Recording Device | Trace production system         |

### Theory

#### Part A: Optical Flat & Interference

**Principle:** Light interference based on path difference

**In-Phase Condition:**

* Path difference = even multiple of half wavelength
* Waves reinforce each other
* Result: Bright spot

**Out-of-Phase Condition:**

* Path difference = odd multiple of half wavelength
* Waves cancel each other
* Result: Dark spot

**Optical Flat Types:**

| Type   | Working Surfaces     | Application                                     |
| ------ | -------------------- | ----------------------------------------------- |
| Type A | Single flat          | Testing precision surfaces (flats, slip gauges) |
| Type B | Both flat & parallel | Testing micrometer anvils, measuring devices    |

**Optical Flat Grades:**

| Grade               | Flatness Tolerance | Parallelism (Type B) | Thickness (Type B) |
| ------------------- | ------------------ | -------------------- | ------------------ |
| Grade I (Reference) | 0.05 µm           | 0.15 µm             | 0.20 µm           |
| Grade II (Working)  | 0.10 µm           | 0.20 µm             | 0.30 µm           |

**Material Composition:**

* Quartz (preferred): Hardness, low expansion, corrosion resistance
* Glass: Good optical properties
* Sapphire: Extreme durability

**Fringe Pattern Interpretation:**

* Straight fringes: Flat surface
* Curved fringes: Surface irregularities
* Fringe count: Indicates roughness magnitude

#### Part B: Surface Roughness Elements

**Surface Classification:**

| Category         | Definition                                              |
| ---------------- | ------------------------------------------------------- |
| Actual Surface   | Physically obtained after manufacturing                 |
| Nominal Surface  | Theoretical perfect surface (average of irregularities) |
| Profile          | Contour of section through surface                      |
| Roughness        | Finely spaced micro-irregularities (3rd & 4th order)    |
| Roughness Height | Arithmetic average deviation (micro-meters)             |
| Roughness Width  | Distance between successive peaks/ridges                |

**Surface Influence on Properties:**

* Appearance
* Corrosion resistance
* Wear resistance
* Fatigue resistance
* Lubrication
* Initial tolerance
* Pressure holding
* Load carrying capacity
* Noise reduction (gears)

#### Measurement Methods

**Method 1: C.L.A. (Center Line Average)**

* Average deviation from nominal surface
* Regardless of arithmetic signs

**Formula:**

```
         h₁ + h₂ + h₃ + ... + hₙ
C.L.A. = ─────────────────────────
                  n

         A₁ + A₂ + A₃ + ... + Aₙ
C.L.A. = ─────────────────────────
                  L

         ΣA
C.L.A. = ────
         L
```

**Advantages of CLA:**

* Easy determination with planimeter
* Readily measured with electrical instruments
* Preferred over RMS for practical measurements

**Method 2: R.M.S. (Root Mean Square)**

* Based on least squares principle
* Square root of arithmetic mean of squared ordinates

**Formula:**

```
         √(y₁² + y₂² + y₃² + ... + yₙ²)
R.M.S. = ────────────────────────────────
                    n
```

**Process:**

1. Divide sample length L into n equal parts
2. Set equidistant ordinates at each point
3. Square each ordinate value
4. Calculate mean of squared values
5. Take square root of mean

### Procedure: Part A (Optical Flat Method)

1. **Cleaning:** Use lint-free paper for both optical flat and workpiece
2. **Placement:** Gently place optical flat on workpiece surface
3. **Air Wedge:** Flat will form slight inclination creating air wedge
4. **Illumination:** Use monochromatic source (sodium vapor light)
5. **Fringe Observation:** View interference fringe pattern
6. **Pattern Analysis:**
   * Straight fringes = flat surface
   * Curved fringes = surface irregularities
7. **Fringe Counting:** Count number and spacing of fringes
8. **Fingertip Test:**
   * Curved fingers brought closer = convex surface
   * Fingers move apart = concave surface

### Procedure: Part B (Stylus Roughness Tester)

1. **Setup:** Place workpiece firmly on measurement table
2. **Stylus Positioning:** Position diamond stylus on surface
3. **Surface Traversal:** Move skid slowly over surface (hand or motor drive)
4. **Datum Reference:** Skid provides surface contour datum
5. **Stylus Movement:** Vertical motion due to surface irregularities
6. **Amplification:** Stylus movements magnified by amplifying device
7. **Trace Production:** Recording device produces surface profile trace
8. **Data Reading:** Record Ra value from digital display
9. **Multi-Location:** Measure at 3+ different locations
10. **Average Calculation:** Calculate average Ra from measurements

### Stylus Type Instrument Components

1. **Skid/Shoe:** Follows surface contours
2. **Finely Pointed Stylus:** Diamond probe for tracing
3. **Amplifying Device:** Magnifies stylus movement
4. **Recording Device:** Produces surface trace
5. **Analysis Means:** Automatic device for trace analysis

### Limitations of Optical Flat

1. Difficult to control optical flat orientation
2. Fringe pattern visibility requires overhead viewing
3. Viewing angle affects measurement accuracy
4. Not suitable for very fine surface details

### Key Points - Part A

* Constant temperature required for accuracy
* Monochromatic light essential for accurate fringe count
* Titanium oxide coating reduces light loss
* Grade I flats for reference, Grade II for working
* Air gap thickness determines fringe pattern

### Key Points - Part B

* Stylus material: Diamond (hardness, durability)
* CLA method preferred over RMS
* Multiple measurement locations ensure representativeness
* Skid provides reference datum
* Trace analysis reveals surface characteristics

### Procedure: Gear Tooth Thickness Measurement

#### Using Gear Tooth Vernier Calliper

1. **Instrument Setup:** Gear tooth vernier has second beam at right angles with tongue
2. **Jaw Alignment:** Tongue slides between jaws at correct distance down flank
3. **Placement:** Place jaws on tooth, tongue resting on top of tooth
4. **Contact:** Ensure jaw tips contact tooth at correct flank position
5. **Reading:** Record vernier calliper reading
6. **Multi-Measurement:** Measure 5 different teeth for variation detection
7. **Calculation:** Average tooth thickness at pitch circle
8. **Comparison:** Compare with theoretical values

#### Chordal Thickness Method (Theoretical)

```
H = h + addendum = R(1 - cos θ) + m
H = (mT/2)[1 - cos(90/T)] + m

W/2 = R sin θ = R sin(360/4T)
W = D sin(90/T)
W_theoretical = mT sin(90/T)
```

Where:

* H = chordal height
* h = height of chord from pitch circle
* m = module
* T = number of teeth
* D = pitch circle diameter

### Procedure: Pitch Circle Diameter Measurement

#### Using Span Measurement (Plate/Flanged Micrometer)

1. **Roller Placement:** Place rollers in tooth spaces at opposite sides
2. **Micrometer Setup:** Position micrometer to measure over rollers
3. **Reading:** Record micrometer reading over span
4. **Calculation:** PCD = M – d, where M = measurement, d = roller diameter
5. **For Even Teeth:**
   * M = D + d
   * D_pcd = M – d
6. **For Odd Teeth:**
   * M = 2 × OP cos(θ/2) + d
   * D_pcd = (M – d)/cos(90/T)
7. **Multiple Positions:** Measure at 3-4 positions around gear
8. **Verification:** Compare with theoretical PCD = m × T

## Procedure: Angular Measurement Using Sine Bar

### Theory

**Principle:** Forms hypotenuse of right-angled triangle

```
sin x = h/l

where:
x = angle
h = height (opposite side)
l = sine bar length (hypotenuse)
x = sin⁻¹(h/l)
```

**Sine Bar Construction:**

* Carefully machined tool steel bar
* Two perfectly scaled cylinders (rollers)
* Same diameter rollers
* Absolutely correct center distance
* Roller centerline parallel to bar edge

### Procedure for Light Workpiece

1. **Approximate Angle:** Find with bevel protractor
2. **Sine Bar Setup:** Set at this approximate angle, clamp to angle plate
3. **Workpiece Mounting:** Clamp workpiece to inclined sine bar
4. **Indicator Setup:** Mount dial indicator on stand with surface gauge
5. **Surface Gauge:** Carry indicator along sine bar surface
6. **Measurement:** Move along sine bar, adjust slip gauges as needed
7. **Variation Recording:** Note any pointer variation
8. **Angular Conversion:** Convert variation to angular measurement
9. **Error Calculation:** tan(error angle) = variation / distance

### Procedure for Heavy Workpiece

1. **Placement:** Place workpiece on surface plate
2. **Sine Bar Position:** Keep sine bar on workpiece
3. **Parallelism:** Make work sine bar work surface parallel to surface plate
4. **Height Measurement:** Measure height over rollers with vernier height gauge
5. **Fiducial Indicator:** Use for constant measurement
6. **Anvil Adjustment:** Adjust height gauge anvil with probe of dial test gauge
7. **Angle Calculation:** x = sin⁻¹(h/l)
   * Where h = height over rollers, l = sine bar length (100 or 200 mm)

### Procedure: Locating Job at Given Angle

1. **Height Calculation:** h = l × sin(x)
   * Where x = required angle, l = sine bar length
2. **Slip Gauge Selection:** Select gauges summing to calculated height
3. **Gauge Placement:** Place slip gauge block above roller
4. **Workpiece Mounting:** Mount job on inclined sine bar
5. **Verification:** Verify angle with dial indicator or bevel protractor

### Sine Bar vs Angle Plates

| Parameter     | Sine Bar           | Angle Plate |
| ------------- | ------------------ | ----------- |
| Accuracy      | Very high          | Good        |
| Angle Range   | Up to 45° (100mm) | Limited     |
| Setup Time    | Moderate           | Quick       |
| Repeatability | Excellent          | Good        |
| Flexibility   | Excellent          | Limited     |

### Key Points

* 100 mm sine bar suitable for small angles (low heights)
* 200 mm sine bar for larger angles
* Slip gauges must be Grade 0 for accuracy
* Surface plate essential for reference
* Dial gauge accuracy critical for deviation measurement

## Key Formulas Index

### Vernier Calliper

```
LC = Main Scale Division / Total Vernier Divisions
LC = 1 mm / 50 = 0.02 mm

Total Reading = MSR + (LC × Coinciding Vernier Division)
```

### Micrometer

```
LC = Pitch / Divisions on Thimble
LC = 0.5 mm / 50 = 0.01 mm

Total Reading = Barrel Reading + (LC × Thimble Division) - Zero Error
```

### Thread Measurement

```
Major Diameter = D + (R2 – R1)

One Wire Method:
Effective Diameter = D = (d1 – d2)

Two Wire Method:
T = S – (R1 – R2)
E = T + P

Constant (P):
Whitworth: P = 0.9605p – 1.1657d
Metric: P = 0.866p – d

Three Wire Method:
E = M – Q
Q = W(1 + cosec θ) – (p/2) cot θ
```

### Best Wire Size

```
db = p/2 sec x

where:
p = thread pitch
x = thread half angle
```

### Dial Gauge Calibration

```
Major Diameter = D + (R2 – R1)

Calibration Range: 0.5 mm to 10 mm
Increment: 0.5 mm steps
```

### Gear Parameters

```
Module = PCD / Number of Teeth
m = D / T

Pitch Circle Diameter:
PCD = m × T

Tooth Thickness (Chordal):
W = D sin(90/T)
H = (mT/2)[1 - cos(90/T)] + m

Center Distance:
a = (m × T₁ + m × T₂) / 2
```

### Surface Roughness

```
C.L.A. Value = ΣA / L

R.M.S. Value = √(y₁² + y₂² + ... + yₙ²) / n
```

### Sine Bar

```
sin x = h / l
x = sin⁻¹(h / l)

where:
x = angle
h = height (slip gauge stack)
l = sine bar length
```

## Measurement Techniques

### Precision Requirements

```
Precision Level    Error Tolerance    Typical Instrument
High              ±0.0025 mm         Micrometer, Floating Carriage
Medium            ±0.02 mm           Vernier Calliper
General           ±0.25 mm           Slide Calipers
```

### Pressure Application

* **Light Pressure:** Micrometers, precision instruments
* **Consistent Pressure:** Use ratchet on micrometer
* **Avoid:** Excessive force causing deformation
* **For Threads:** Gentle pressure to prevent crest damage

### Multiple Measurement Strategy

```
Standard Approach:
1. Measure at 3 different linear positions (detect taper)
2. Measure at 2-3 angular positions (detect ovality)
3. Calculate average
4. Analyze pattern for systematic errors
```

### Temperature Compensation

* Most critical for precision measurements
* Optical flats: Constant temperature area required
* Slip gauges: Calibration correction factors applied
* General rule: ±1°C can cause ±0.01 mm variation

### Measurement Sequence (Best Practice)

1. Verify instrument zero/calibration first
2. Take initial reading
3. Remove workpiece
4. Reposition workpiece independently
5. Take second reading
6. Repeat for third measurement
7. Compare readings (validate consistency)
8. Report average with uncertainty

## Common Errors & Solutions

### Measurement Errors

| Error Type        | Cause                           | Solution                             |
| ----------------- | ------------------------------- | ------------------------------------ |
| Parallax Error    | Viewing angle not perpendicular | View directly perpendicular to scale |
| Zero Error        | Instrument not calibrated       | Check zero before each use           |
| Pressure Error    | Inconsistent measuring force    | Use ratchet stop on micrometer       |
| Temperature Error | Thermal expansion/contraction   | Measure in constant temperature area |
| Mechanical Wear   | Component wear over time        | Calibrate periodically               |

### Instrument-Specific Errors

#### Vernier Calliper

* **Worn sliding mechanism:** Replace instrument
* **Blunt measuring tips:** Sharpen or replace
* **Dirty scale:** Clean with soft cloth
* **Solution:** Use light pressure, smooth sliding motion

#### Micrometer

* **Zero error:** Record and subtract from readings
* **Backlash:** Always approach from same direction
* **Worn screw:** Professional servicing required
* **Solution:** Use ratchet for consistent pressure

#### Dial Gauge

* **Needle sticking:** Tap gently, clean mechanism
* **Scale drift:** Recalibrate with slip gauges
* **Loose needle:** Tighten retaining nut
* **Solution:** Periodic calibration (monthly/quarterly)

#### Floating Carriage Micrometer

* **Alignment error:** Check alignment before use
* **Wire contamination:** Clean wires thoroughly
* **Anvil wear:** Professional inspection needed
* **Solution:** Gentle, consistent pressure application

#### Optical Flat

* **Dust contamination:** Clean with lint-free paper
* **Coating damage:** Handle only when necessary
* **Temperature drift:** Allow thermal equilibration
* **Solution:** Store in protective case, clean carefully

#### Stylus Tester

* **Stylus wear:** Replace diamond stylus if needed
* **Calibration drift:** Verify with reference standard
* **Surface contamination:** Clean before measurement
* **Solution:** Regular maintenance schedule

### Data Analysis Errors

| Error                         | Impact                 | Prevention                        |
| ----------------------------- | ---------------------- | --------------------------------- |
| Not taking averages           | Unreliable results     | Minimum 3 measurements            |
| Ignoring outliers             | Skewed averages        | Investigate unusual readings      |
| No precision specification    | Measurement ambiguity  | Always state precision/LC         |
| Single measurement            | No confidence interval | Multiple independent measurements |
| Environmental factors ignored | Systematic bias        | Control temperature, humidity     |

## Quick Decision Trees

### Choosing the Right Instrument

#### For Dimensions < 10 mm

```
START: Dimension needed?
├─ Internal dimension?
│  └─ YES → Vernier Calliper or Digital Vernier
└─ External dimension?
   └─ < 1 mm precision needed?
      ├─ YES → Micrometer (LC: 0.01 mm)
      └─ NO → Vernier Calliper (LC: 0.02 mm)
```

#### For Thread Measurement

```
START: Thread parameter needed?
├─ Major diameter?
│  └─ Ordinary or Bench Micrometer
├─ Effective diameter?
│  └─ Accuracy level?
│     ├─ High → Three wire method
│     ├─ Medium → Two wire method
│     └─ Low → Thread micrometer method
```

#### For Surface Analysis

```
START: Surface quality assessment?
├─ Visual inspection needed?
│  └─ → Optical flat with interferometer
├─ Quantitative roughness?
│  └─ → Stylus roughness tester
└─ Both?
   └─ → Complete analysis (both methods)
```

#### For Angular Measurement

```
START: Angle measurement?
├─ Quick approximate angle?
│  └─ → Bevel protractor (LC: 5')
├─ High precision angle?
│  └─ Angle < 45°?
│     ├─ YES → Sine bar (100 mm)
│     └─ NO → Sine bar (200 mm)
└─ Set component at angle?
   └─ → Sine bar with slip gauges
```

## Laboratory Safety & Best Practices

### General Precautions

* Handle all instruments with care
* Avoid dropping precision instruments
* Never force any measurement tool
* Keep instruments clean and dry
* Store in protective cases
* Verify zero before each measurement

### Instrument-Specific Safety

#### Micrometers & Vernier

* Never rotate beyond smooth contact
* Use ratchet stop (not barrel directly)
* Avoid excessive finger pressure
* Clean tips before measurement

#### Dial Gauge & Indicator

* Protect needle from shock loads
* Allow gentle contact only
* Check zero regularly
* Avoid temperature extremes

#### Optical Flats

* Handle with extreme care
* Use only lint-free cleaning
* Store horizontally in protective case
* Keep away from dust and heat

#### Electrical Instruments (Stylus Tester)

* Protect from moisture
* Shut down properly

* Include instrument serial number
* Document any instrument issues
* Maintain consistent format

## Troubleshooting Guide

### Issue: Inconsistent Readings

**Check these in order:**

1. Instrument calibration (verify zero)
2. Measurement technique (pressure, alignment)
3. Environmental conditions (temperature, vibration)
4. Workpiece contact surface (cleanliness, damage)
5. Operator technique (angle, consistency)

### Issue: Cannot Obtain Zero Reading

**For Micrometers:**

* Record zero error, apply correction to all readings
* Professional calibration if > 0.02 mm error

**For Dial Gauges:**

* Use adjustment screw under dust cap
* Verify against slip gauge reference

**For Digital Instruments:**

* Check battery level
* Verify zero button function
* Reset if persistent issue

### Issue: Instrument Won't Move Smoothly

**Check:**

1. Cleanliness of moving parts
2. Mechanical obstruction
3. Worn internal components
4. Need for lubrication (specific instruments only)

## Performance Metrics & Acceptability Criteria

### Measurement Accuracy

```
Excellent: ±0.01 mm (0.3% of reading)
Good:      ±0.02 mm (0.5% of reading)
Acceptable: ±0.05 mm (1% of reading)
Poor:      > ±0.05 mm
```

### Repeatability

```
Excellent: All readings within 0.02 mm
Good:      All readings within 0.05 mm
Acceptable: All readings within 0.10 mm
Investigate: Variation > 0.10 mm
```

### Temperature Stability

```
Ideal:     ±1°C constant
Acceptable: ±3°C variation
Monitor:   >±3°C variation
Control:   Required if > ±5°C variation
```
